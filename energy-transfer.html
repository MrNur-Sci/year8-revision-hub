1. Energy Stores

Energy is stored in different ways. When a system changes, energy is transferred between stores. Energy is not created or destroyed; it is transferred, stored or dissipated to the surroundings.

🔋 Chemical
Food, fuels, batteries.
🏃 Kinetic
Moving objects.
🌡️ Thermal
Hot objects. Higher temperature usually means more energy in the thermal store.
⬆️ Gravitational potential
Objects lifted higher.
🌀 Elastic potential
Stretched/compressed springs, elastic bands.
⚛️ Nuclear
Atomic nuclei, nuclear fuels.
Best scientific language: “Energy is transferred from the chemical store to the thermal store.” Avoid: “energy is used up” or “energy disappears”.

2. Changes in Energy Stores

Use this model to link situations to energy store changes.

Starting storegravitational potential
Ending storekinetic + thermal
Falling object: gravitational potential energy store decreases. Kinetic energy store increases. Some energy is dissipated to the thermal store due to air resistance.

3. Energy in Food Practical

Food stores energy chemically. In a simple food test, a burning food sample heats water. The temperature change of the water gives evidence that energy has been transferred from the chemical store of the food to the thermal store of the water and surroundings.

Food samplecrisp
Temperature change22 °C
Conclusionmedium energy
Crisp: temperature rise is moderate. More temperature rise suggests more energy transferred to the water, but some energy is always lost to the surroundings.
Variable typeExample in this practical
Independent variableType or mass of food sample
Dependent variableTemperature change of the water
Control variablesVolume of water, starting temperature, distance from flame to tube, mass of food if comparing energy per gram

4. Power

Power is the rate of energy transfer. A device with a higher power transfers energy faster.

Equation: power = energy transferred ÷ time. Unit: watt, W. 1 W = 1 J/s.
Energy transferred100 J
Time10 s
Power10 W
Power = 100 ÷ 10 = 10 W.

5. Sankey Diagrams

A Sankey diagram shows energy transfers. Wider arrows show larger amounts of energy. Useful energy goes in the desired direction. Wasted energy is dissipated to the surroundings.

Input energy100 J
Useful energy60 J
Wasted energy40 J
Useful + wasted energy = input energy. If input is 100 J and useful is 60 J, wasted energy is 40 J.

6. Efficiency

Efficiency tells us what fraction or percentage of input energy is transferred usefully.

Efficiency equation: efficiency = useful output energy ÷ total input energy × 100%
Useful energy60 J
Input energy100 J
Efficiency60%
Efficiency = 60 ÷ 100 × 100 = 60%.

7. Renewable and Non-renewable Energy Resources

Energy resources are used to generate electricity, provide heating and power transport. They have advantages and disadvantages.

Renewable resources

Replaced naturally in a short time.

  • Solar
  • Wind
  • Hydroelectric
  • Geothermal
  • Biofuel
Usually lower carbon emissions during generation, but may be affected by weather, location or cost.

Non-renewable resources

Used faster than they are replaced.

  • Coal
  • Oil
  • Natural gas
  • Nuclear fuel
Fossil fuels release carbon dioxide when burned. Nuclear power does not release carbon dioxide during generation but produces radioactive waste.
ResourceAdvantageDisadvantage
WindRenewable, no fuel burnedDepends on wind conditions
SolarRenewable, useful in sunny placesOutput changes with light level
Coal/gas/oilReliable, high energy outputNon-renewable and releases carbon dioxide
NuclearReliable, high energy output, low carbon during generationRadioactive waste and expensive power stations
