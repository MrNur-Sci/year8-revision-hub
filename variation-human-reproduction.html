1. Reproductive Organs

Learn The reproductive system contains organs that produce sex cells and support fertilisation and development of a baby.

Female reproductive system

OrganFunction
OvaryProduces eggs and releases hormones.

3. Fertilisation

Learn Fertilisation happens when the nucleus of a sperm cell joins with the nucleus of an egg cell. This forms a fertilised egg cell called a zygote.

KeywordMeaning
GameteA sex cell, such as sperm or egg.
FertilisationFusion of sperm and egg nuclei.
ZygoteThe fertilised egg cell formed after fertilisation.
EmbryoEarly stage of development after the zygote divides.

4. Stages of Pregnancy

Learn After fertilisation, the zygote divides to form an embryo. The embryo implants in the uterus lining. Later, it develops into a foetus.

StructureFunction
PlacentaAllows exchange of substances between mother and foetus.
Umbilical cordConnects foetus to placenta.
Amniotic fluidCushions and protects the foetus.
UterusWhere the embryo/foetus develops.
The mother's blood and the foetus's blood do not normally mix directly; substances diffuse across the placenta.

5. Infertility Issues

Learn Infertility means difficulty becoming pregnant. It can be linked to sperm, eggs, hormones, blocked tubes or other medical factors.

Sperm-related

Low sperm count or sperm not moving properly can make fertilisation less likely.

Egg or ovulation-related

If ovulation does not happen regularly, fewer eggs are available for fertilisation.

Blocked tubes

If oviducts are blocked, egg and sperm may not meet.

This is a science learning section. Real infertility is a medical issue and should be discussed with qualified health professionals.

6. Variation

Learn Variation means differences between organisms of the same species. Variation can be caused by genes, the environment, or both.

Genetic variation

Differences caused by inherited genes, such as blood group.

Environmental variation

Differences caused by surroundings or lifestyle, such as a scar.

Both

Some characteristics are affected by genes and environment, such as height or body mass.

7. Continuous and Discontinuous Variation

Learn Continuous variation has values across a range, usually measured with numbers. Discontinuous variation has distinct categories.

Continuous variation

Examples: height, hand span, body mass. Data often forms a range and may be shown on a line graph or histogram.

Discontinuous variation

Examples: blood group, tongue rolling category, attached/free earlobes. Data is counted in categories and often shown as a bar chart.
