1. Work Done

Work done = force × distance. Work is done when a force makes an object move in the direction of the force. Work done is measured in joules, J.

2. Heating Water: Temperature, Kinetic Energy and Internal Energy

This model shows water being heated from -20 °C to 120 °C. Move the temperature slider. The particles and graph cursor update together. When temperature increases, particle kinetic energy increases. During melting/boiling, temperature and kinetic energy stay constant, but internal energy increases because particle arrangement changes.

Key idea: At the melting point/freezing point, water changes between solid ice and liquid water. At the boiling point/condensation point, water changes between liquid water and gas/steam. During these flat parts of the graph, the temperature stays constant, so average particle kinetic energy stays constant. Energy is still being transferred, but it increases internal energy by changing the arrangement of particles.

3. Conduction

Conduction transfers thermal energy through solids. Particles in a solid stay in fixed positions. They vibrate and pass energy to neighbouring particles. Metals are good conductors; wood is a poor conductor.

Materialmetal
Heat inputmedium
Observationenergy spreads far along the metal
Metal transfers thermal energy quickly because it is a good conductor.

4. Convection

Convection happens in fluids: liquids and gases. Hot air expands, becomes less dense and rises. Cooler, denser air sinks to replace it.

A convection current forms because warm air rises and cooler air sinks.

5. Radiation Practical

Radiation transfers thermal energy by infrared waves. It does not need particles. Dark, matt surfaces are good absorbers and emitters. Light, shiny surfaces reflect more radiation.

Surfaceblack matt
Absorptionhigh
Temperature riselarge
Black matt surfaces absorb more infrared radiation, so their temperature increases more quickly.
